Sundarban
About

The Sundarbans Reserve Forest (SRF), located in the south-west of Bangladesh between the river Baleswar in the East and the Harinbanga in the West, adjoining to the Bay of Bengal, is the largest contiguous mangrove forest in the world. Lying between latitude 21° 27′ 30″ and 22° 30′ 00″ North and longitude 89° 02′ 00″ and 90° 00′ 00″ East and with a total area of 10,000 km2, 60% of the property lies in Bangladesh and the rest in India. The land area, including exposed sandbars, occupies 414,259 ha (70%) with water bodies covering 187,413 ha (30%). The three wildlife sanctuaries in the south cover an area of 139,700 ha and are considered core breeding areas for a number of endangered species. Situated in a unique bioclimatic zone within a typical geographical situation in the coastal region of the Bay of Bengal, it is a landmark of ancient heritage of mythological and historical events. Bestowed with magnificent scenic beauty and natural resources, it is internationally recognized for its high biodiversity of mangrove flora and fauna both on land and water. The immense tidal mangrove forests of Bangladeshs’ Sundarbans Forest Reserve, is in reality a mosaic of islands of different shapes and sizes, perennially washed by brackish water shrilling in and around the endless and mind-boggling labyrinths of water channels. The site supports exceptional biodiversity in its terrestrial, aquatic and marine habitats; ranging from micro to macro flora and fauna. The Sundarbans is of universal importance for globally endangered species including the Royal Bengal Tiger, Ganges and Irawadi dolphins, estuarine crocodiles and the critically endangered endemic river terrapin (Batagur baska). It is the only mangrove habitat in the world for Panthera tigris tigris species.

Challenges

The Sundarbans presents a range of challenges due to its unique environment. Harsh climate conditions, such as monsoon rains and cyclones, create difficulty for both residents and visitors. Rising sea levels and riverbank erosion contribute to flooding and habitat loss. Wildlife-human conflicts, especially with Bengal tigers, pose safety concerns. Limited infrastructure, including access to clean water and sanitation, hinders development. Overfishing and deforestation disrupt the delicate ecosystem. The area's vulnerability to climate change, coupled with health risks and limited economic opportunities, further compound the challenges. Finding a balance between conservation and sustainable development, alongside effective governance and connectivity, is crucial for addressing these complex issues in the Sundarbans.

Climate

The Sundarbans, nestled within the Bay of Bengal's delta region, boasts a climate that encapsulates the essence of the tropics. Its distinctive geographical location and proximity to the sea profoundly influence its weather patterns. Encompassing both India and Bangladesh, this remarkable mangrove ecosystem experiences a tropical climate that is both warm and humid throughout the year. The monsoon season, spanning from June to September, ushers in heavy rainfall carried by the southwest monsoon winds from the Bay of Bengal. This period witnesses abundant precipitation that can lead to flooding. Temperature variations remain relatively modest due to its coastal presence, maintaining an average range of 25°C to 35°C (77°F to 95°F) year-round. The high humidity levels, surpassing 80% during monsoons, contribute to the region's perpetually damp environment. Notably, the Sundarbans contends with the occasional onslaught of tropical cyclones, accompanied by torrential rains, powerful winds, and storm surges, causing significant ecological and human impact. Its distinctive blend of freshwater river influx and the saline embrace of the Bay of Bengal shapes varying salinity levels, intricately shaping the distribution of flora and fauna. Among the diverse array of mangrove trees, aquatic life, and wildlife such as Bengal tigers and crocodiles, the climate plays an instrumental role, influencing their survival strategies. Regrettably, the vulnerability of the Sundarbans to sea-level rise exacerbates the threat posed by climate change. As sea levels rise, the region faces heightened salinity levels, land erosion, and habitat loss, underscoring the delicate interplay between the Sundarbans' unique climate and its precious ecosystem.

Tourists

Tourists visit the Sundarbans throughout the year, but the peak tourist seasons are generally during the winter months and the drier part of the year. The best time to visit the Sundarbans for tourism is typically from October to March. During this period, the weather is more pleasant as the monsoon season has ended, resulting in lower humidity and milder temperatures. This makes it a more comfortable experience for tourists to explore the mangrove forests and engage in various activities. The monsoon season, from June to September, is generally avoided by tourists due to the heavy rainfall, high humidity, and potential for flooding. The hot and humid summer months, from April to June, might also see fewer tourists due to the discomfort caused by the weather. It's important to note that the exact timing of tourist visits can vary based on factors like local festivals, holidays, and personal preferences. If you're planning to visit the Sundarbans, it's advisable to check with local tour operators or travel agencies for the most up-to-date information on the best times to visit based on your preferences and the specific activities you'd like to engage in during your trip.
